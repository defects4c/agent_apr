# Eight prompting baselines: verified against the papers

**Six of the eight baselines are legitimate, well-documented prompting techniques with traceable academic origins, but every description contains at least one meaningful inaccuracy.** The most common error is oversimplification — reducing multi-component frameworks to single-sentence summaries that miss defining features. Two baselines warrant special flags: "Regular Prompting" lacks a formal academic term, and "Function Calling" is not a prompting technique at all but an API-level capability involving model fine-tuning. The list also omits **Self-Consistency** (Wang et al., 2022), arguably the single most important prompting baseline in the reasoning literature.

---

## 1. Regular Prompting is informal shorthand, not an academic term

**Claimed:** Direct question, no scaffold.

**Verdict: Partially correct, but imprecise.** The academic literature uses **"standard prompting"** (Wei et al., 2022) or **"vanilla prompting,"** never "regular prompting." More critically, the description conflates two distinct baselines. **Zero-shot standard prompting** gives the model only the question — matching the "direct question, no scaffold" description. **Few-shot standard prompting** provides input-output exemplars but without reasoning chains. Wei et al.'s CoT paper uses the few-shot variant as its primary baseline, while Kojima et al. use the zero-shot variant. Any rigorous evaluation should specify which form is meant, as performance differences between them are substantial.

**Correction needed:** Replace "Regular Prompting" with "Standard Prompting" and specify zero-shot versus few-shot.

---

## 2. ReAct's Thought/Action/Observation loop is accurate, but the year needs clarification

**Claimed:** Thought/Action/Observation loop, Yao et al. 2022.

**Verdict: Mechanism is correct; year is ambiguous.** The paper "ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models" by Shunyu Yao, Jeffrey Zhao, Dian Yu, Nan Du, Izhak Shafran, Karthik Narasimhan, and Yuan Cao appeared on arXiv in **October 2022** (2210.03629) but was formally published at **ICLR 2023**. The official BibTeX uses `year = {2023}`. Citing "Yao et al. 2022" is defensible for the preprint but technically incorrect for the published version.

The **Thought/Action/Observation** interleaving is precisely what the paper describes. **Thought** is a verbal reasoning trace generated by the LLM for planning and reflection. **Action** is a task-specific command the LLM selects (e.g., `Search[query]`, `Lookup[term]`, `Finish[answer]`). **Observation** is the environment's response — critically, this is *not* generated by the LLM but injected from external tools like Wikipedia APIs. The prompt uses **few-shot in-context examples** (typically 1–2 human-written trajectories). The paper's core insight is that reasoning traces help the model plan and handle exceptions, while actions ground it in real information to reduce hallucination.

**Best use cases:** Multi-hop QA (HotpotQA), fact verification (FEVER), interactive decision-making (ALFWorld, WebShop).

**Correction needed:** Cite as Yao et al. (2023) for the published ICLR version. Note that Observations come from the environment, not the LLM.

---

## 3. Chain-of-Thought conflates two distinct papers with different mechanisms

**Claimed:** "Let's think step by step," Wei et al. 2022 and Kojima et al. 2022.

**Verdict: The two papers are correctly identified, but the description misleadingly merges them.** This is the most significant inaccuracy in the list. "Let's think step by step" comes exclusively from **Kojima et al.** (zero-shot CoT), not from Wei et al.

**Wei et al. (2022)** — "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models" (arXiv January 2022, **NeurIPS 2022**) — introduced **few-shot CoT**. The technique provides manually written ⟨question, step-by-step reasoning chain, answer⟩ exemplars. No trigger phrase is used. The model learns the reasoning pattern from demonstrations and generates its own chain of thought for the test question.

**Kojima et al. (2022)** — "Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Reasoners" (arXiv May 2022, **NeurIPS 2022**) — introduced **zero-shot CoT** using the simple trigger phrase **"Let's think step by step"** appended to the question, requiring no exemplars. Importantly, this uses a **two-stage process**: first elicit reasoning with the trigger phrase, then extract the final answer with a second prompt like "Therefore, the answer is."

Both papers found CoT is an **emergent ability** effective only in large models (~100B+ parameters) — it can actually hurt smaller models. Wei et al. came first chronologically and is the origin of the "Chain-of-Thought" concept.

**Correction needed:** Clearly distinguish few-shot CoT (Wei et al.) from zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al.). The phrase "Let's think step by step" belongs solely to Kojima et al.

## 4. Reflexion is far more than a self-critique loop

**Claimed:** Self-critique and revision loop, Shinn et al. 2023.

**Verdict: Year and attribution are correct; the mechanism description is significantly oversimplified.** The paper "Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning" by Noah Shinn, Federico Cassano, Ashwin Gopinath, Karthik Narasimhan, and Shunyu Yao (arXiv March 2023, **NeurIPS 2023**) describes a **multi-trial verbal reinforcement learning framework**, not a simple self-critique loop.

The framework has **four distinct components**: an **Actor** (LLM generating actions, potentially using CoT or ReAct), an **Evaluator** (scoring outputs via heuristics or LLM-based classification), a **Self-Reflection model** (generating natural language summaries of failures), and **episodic Memory** (storing reflections across trials in a sliding window buffer). The agent operates across **multiple sequential trials** — attempting a task, receiving *external* feedback (from compilers, test suites, or environment signals), reflecting, storing the reflection, and retrying. This is fundamentally different from single-pass self-refinement methods like Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023). The paper's own ablation shows self-reflection provides an **8% absolute improvement** over memory alone.

**Best use cases:** Code generation (achieved **91% pass@1** on HumanEval, surpassing GPT-4's 80% at the time), sequential decision-making (ALFWorld), and multi-hop QA (HotPotQA).

**Correction needed:** Describe as "multi-trial verbal RL framework with episodic memory and external feedback," not just "self-critique and revision loop." Emphasize the multi-trial nature and the four-component architecture.

## 5. Tree of Thoughts involves search algorithms, not just branching

**Claimed:** Multiple reasoning branches, Yao et al. 2023.

**Verdict: Attribution is correct; description captures only one of four key components.** The paper "Tree of Thoughts: Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models" by Shunyu Yao, Dian Yu, Jeffrey Zhao, Izhak Shafran, Thomas L. Griffiths, Yuan Cao, and Karthik Narasimhan (arXiv May 2023, **NeurIPS 2023 oral**) defines four explicit design decisions, not just branching:

1. **Thought decomposition** — defining the granularity of intermediate steps (words, equations, paragraphs)
2. **Thought generation** — creating candidates via sampling (i.i.d. from CoT) or proposing (sequential generation)
3. **State evaluation** — LLM-based heuristics scoring each state as "sure/maybe/impossible" (value) or comparative voting across states (vote)
4. **Search algorithm** — classical **BFS or DFS** with **lookahead and backtracking**

The crucial missing elements from the description are **evaluation** and **backtracking**. ToT doesn't just branch — it evaluates intermediate states and abandons unpromising paths. This is inspired by Newell, Shaw, and Simon's 1950s characterization of problem-solving as search through combinatorial spaces. The paper proves that standard prompting, CoT, and CoT with self-consistency are all **special cases** of ToT (trees with limited depth/breadth). On the Game of 24 benchmark, ToT achieved **74% success versus 4% for GPT-4 with CoT**.

**Correction needed:** Add evaluation and search algorithms (BFS/DFS with backtracking) to the description. "Multiple reasoning branches" alone misses the defining features.

## 6. Graph of Thoughts is correctly described but the key innovation is aggregation

**Claimed:** Graph-structured reasoning with nodes and edges, Besta et al. 2023.

**Verdict: Broadly correct, but the year and defining innovation need clarification.** "Graph of Thoughts: Solving Elaborate Problems with Large Language Models" by Maciej Besta, Nils Blach, and nine co-authors from ETH Zurich and collaborating institutions was posted on arXiv in **August 2023** but formally published at **AAAI 2024**. The correct venue citation is Besta et al., AAAI 2024.

GoT is formally defined as a tuple **(G, T, E, R)** — graph, transformations, evaluator, ranking. What makes it distinct from ToT are three **thought transformations**: **Generation** (standard branching), **Aggregation** (merging multiple thoughts into one — structurally impossible in a tree), and **Refinement** (self-loops for iterative improvement). The **aggregation** operation is the key novelty, enabling synergy across reasoning branches that trees cannot achieve. The system architecture consists of a Controller (managing the Graph of Operations and Graph Reasoning State), a Prompter, a Parser, and a Scoring Module. CoT, CoT-SC, and ToT are all proven to be special cases within GoT's framework.

**Best use cases:** Sorting (**62% quality improvement** over ToT with >31% cost reduction), set operations, document merging — generally tasks naturally decomposable into subtasks that benefit from being merged.

**Correction needed:** Cite as AAAI 2024 for the published version. Emphasize that aggregation (merging branches) is the defining innovation over ToT.

## 7. Program of Thoughts disentangles computation from reasoning, not just "writes code"

**Claimed:** Model writes executable code, Chen et al. 2022.

**Verdict: Core idea is correct but oversimplified; year is ambiguous.** "Program of Thoughts Prompting: Disentangling Computation from Reasoning for Numerical Reasoning Tasks" by Wenhu Chen, Xueguang Ma, Xinyi Wang, and William W. Cohen appeared on arXiv in **November 2022** (2211.12588) but was formally published in **TMLR 2023**. The key innovation is not just code generation but **disentangling computation from reasoning** — the LLM handles decomposition and logical structure while an external Python interpreter handles arithmetic. Generated programs interleave natural language comments (explaining reasoning) with executable Python code, specifically leveraging **SymPy** for symbolic mathematics.

A critical omission is the concurrent work **PAL: Program-aided Language Models** (Gao et al., arXiv November 18, 2022 — just four days before PoT — published at **ICML 2023**). PAL and PoT independently developed the same core idea. PAL uses a function-based code style (`def solution():`) across a broader task range, while PoT specifically leverages SymPy for symbolic math and focuses on numerical/financial reasoning. Both are widely cited; omitting PAL when including PoT is a notable gap.

**Correction needed:** Emphasize the disentangling of computation from reasoning, mention SymPy usage, and acknowledge PAL as a concurrent parallel contribution.

## 8. Function Calling is not a prompting technique

**Claimed:** Structured tool use with JSON schemas, baseline for tool-using agents.

**Verdict: The mechanism description is accurate, but classifying it as a "prompting baseline" is incorrect.** Function calling was introduced by **OpenAI on June 13, 2023** as an API feature for GPT-3.5-turbo and GPT-4 models. It is **not based on any single academic paper** and involves **model fine-tuning**, not prompt engineering. The mechanism works as described — developers define available functions via JSON Schema, the model outputs structured JSON with function names and arguments, and the application layer executes the functions — but the model's ability to do this comes from training, not prompting.

The "Prompt Report" survey (Schulhoff et al., 2024), which catalogued **58 text-only prompting techniques**, does not include function calling among them. Related academic work includes **Toolformer** (Schick et al., NeurIPS 2023), which uses self-supervised training for tool use, and **Gorilla** (Patil et al., 2023), which fine-tunes LLaMA for API calls. All of these involve training modifications, not prompting. Function calling is better categorized as a **tool-use/agentic capability** and is a legitimate baseline for evaluating agent systems (e.g., the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard), but not for evaluating prompting techniques.

**Correction needed:** Reclassify as an API-level tool-use capability, not a prompting baseline. Note the absence of a founding academic paper.

## Are these "widely accepted prompting baselines"? What's missing?

Six of the eight — CoT, ReAct, ToT, GoT, PoT, and Reflexion — are legitimate prompting or reasoning frameworks regularly appearing in academic surveys and benchmarks. Standard/Regular Prompting is a recognized control condition rather than a technique. Function Calling belongs to a different category entirely.

The most significant omissions from this list are **Self-Consistency** (Wang et al., 2022), which uses majority voting across multiple sampled reasoning paths and delivers massive gains (e.g., **+17.9% on GSM8K** over standard CoT); **Least-to-Most Prompting** (Zhou et al., 2022), which decomposes complex problems into sequential sub-problems; and **PAL** (Gao et al., 2022), the concurrent twin of PoT. Any comprehensive baseline list for prompting research should include Self-Consistency — its omission is the single largest gap.

## Conclusion

The eight baselines form a reasonable but imperfect survey of the prompting landscape. The recurring pattern across all descriptions is **reductive oversimplification** — collapsing multi-component frameworks into single phrases that miss their defining innovations. Reflexion is not just self-critique (it's multi-trial RL with memory). ToT is not just branching (it's search with evaluation and backtracking). PoT is not just code generation (it's computational disentangling with symbolic math). Publication years are consistently ambiguous because most papers have arXiv dates 6–12 months before their conference proceedings. For rigorous citation, the formal venue and year should be preferred: ReAct at ICLR 2023, CoT at NeurIPS 2022, Reflexion and ToT at NeurIPS 2023, GoT at AAAI 2024, and PoT at TMLR 2023. Adding Self-Consistency and replacing Function Calling with PAL would produce a more academically defensible baseline set.
